The challenge is real

A Gaussian prior is fully supported; data rarely is. Something has to break down as $t \to 1$.

Vector field near terminal time $$u_t(y)=\frac{\dot{\beta}_t}{\beta_t}\,y+\frac{\dot{\alpha}_t\beta_t-\alpha_t\dot{\beta}_t}{\beta_t}\,\E[X\mid X_t=y]$$ As $t \to 1$, $\beta_t \to 0$ — the denominator creates a genuine blow-up risk at the endpoint.
Prop. C.4 — Elucidating, Appendix C.2 Let $\Omega = \supp(p)$ and $\sigma_\Omega$ its medial axis. If $\Omega = \R^d$ with Lipschitz density, $u_t$ stays bounded on $[0,1)$. If $\Omega \neq \R^d$, then $\lim_{t\to 1}\|u_t(x)\|=\infty$ for every $x\notin\Omega\cup\sigma_\Omega$.
Textbook ODE warning

Many ODE techniques we learn from textbooks rely on a Lipschitz vector field. We do not have that here. So the earlier continuity-equation story for $(u_t,p_t)$ was slightly lying to you.

There really is a terminal-time singularity when the data support is not full. Textbook ODE tools would be comfortable if the field were Lipschitz; here the point is that the endpoint singularity removes that comfort.
